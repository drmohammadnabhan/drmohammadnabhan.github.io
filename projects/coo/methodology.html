Methodology

The Snell-KKT Identity and the COO Algorithm

The Core Insight

The paper’s intellectual contribution rests on a single observation: when written in vector form for \(n\) dimensions, the Snell law of elastic reflection and the KKT first-order optimality conditions are the same equation. This is not an analogy — it is an algebraic identity, derived from first principles by imposing two simultaneous requirements on any boundary operator.

The Snell-KKT Operator

At a point \(x\) on the constraint boundary \(G_i(x) = 0\), we require a momentum update mapping incoming momentum \(p^-\) to outgoing momentum \(p^+\) satisfying:

Requirement 1 — Normality (KKT stationarity). The momentum change must be along the constraint gradient: \[p^+ - p^- = \lambda_i \nabla G_i(x)\]

From optimization: KKT stationarity requires the net impulse at the boundary to be normal to the constraint surface. From physics: a frictionless wall exerts only a normal force. Both give the same structural form.

Requirement 2 — Hamiltonian conservation. Total energy is unchanged: \[\|p^+\|^2 = \|p^-\|^2\]

Substituting the first requirement into the second and solving for the scalar \(\lambda_i\) yields the unique non-trivial solution:

\[\boxed{p^+ = p^- + \lambda_i \nabla G_i(x), \qquad \lambda_i = -\frac{2\langle p^-, \nabla G_i(x)\rangle}{\|\nabla G_i(x)\|^2}}\]

This is the Snell-KKT operator. The scalar \(\lambda_i\) it produces is simultaneously the elastic impulse coefficient that reflects the trajectory and the KKT multiplier (shadow price) for constraint \(i\) — generated as a byproduct of enforcing Hamiltonian conservation at the constraint wall.

What the Operator Guarantees

Lemma 2 establishes four properties of the Snell-KKT operator, satisfied exactly (not approximately):

  1. KKT stationarity: \(\Delta p = \lambda_i \nabla G_i(x)\) — momentum change is normal to the constraint surface
  2. Hamiltonian conservation: \(H(x, p^+) = H(x, p^-)\) — proved algebraically, not approximately
  3. Dual feasibility: \(\lambda_i \geq 0\) when the trajectory arrives from the feasible interior
  4. Complementary slackness: \(\lambda_i = 0\) for all inactive constraints

These are precisely the four KKT conditions, satisfied at every boundary event throughout the algorithm’s run — not just at the terminal point.

Historical Context

The Snell-KKT identity is not an ad hoc construction. It is the conclusion of a four-century trajectory of variational thinking about light-surface interaction: from Heron of Alexandria (c. 60 CE), who showed reflected light takes the minimum total path, through Fermat’s principle (1657), to the modern vector formulation in Born and Wolf (1999). The optimization connection arises because both fields — geometric optics and constrained optimization — derive from the same variational framework.

The Algorithm

COO operates by alternating two phases governed by different integrators, with a statistical trigger managing the switch.

Phase 1 — Lumen (Conservative Exploration)

The Lumen phase uses the Störmer-Verlet (leapfrog) integrator — the standard symplectic integrator for Hamiltonian systems. One step with step size \(s = 1/L\) (where \(L\) is the Lipschitz constant of \(\nabla f\)):

\[p_{k+1/2} = p_k - \frac{s}{2}\nabla f(x_k)\] \[x_{k+1} = x_k + s \cdot p_{k+1/2}\] \[p_{k+1} = p_{k+1/2} - \frac{s}{2}\nabla f(x_{k+1})\]

When the proposed position \(x_{k+1}\) would violate constraint \(i\), the Snell-KKT operator is applied to \(p_{k+1/2}\) before the position update. Crucially, \(x_k\) is never modified during boundary handling — only the momentum is reflected. The position update then proceeds with the reflected momentum, keeping the trajectory feasible.

The Störmer-Verlet integrator is equivalent to Nesterov’s Accelerated Gradient Descent at the continuous-time limit (Zhang et al., 2021; Campos et al., 2023). This equivalence is the foundation of Theorem 1’s \(o(1/k^3)\) gradient norm rate.

The Luminosity Flux

Each Snell-KKT boundary event at step \(j\) emits a multiplier \(\lambda_i^{(j)}\). These are accumulated into the Luminosity Flux:

\[\bar{\Lambda}_k = \frac{1}{N_k} \sum_{j=1}^{N_k} \lambda_i^{(j)}\]

where \(N_k\) counts Snell-KKT events (not Householder fallback events) up to step \(k\). Theorem 5 establishes \(\bar{\Lambda}_k \to \lambda_i^*\) at \(O(1/k^2)\).

Trigger — Adaptive Percentile Filter (APF)

The APF monitors the rolling Hamiltonian history and fires the Ombra phase when three conditions hold simultaneously:

  1. Sufficient history has accumulated (\(|\text{history}| \geq 20\))
  2. The current Hamiltonian is anomalously low: \(H_{k+1} < \text{Percentile}_\tau(\text{history})\)
  3. A cooldown period has elapsed: \(k - k_{\text{last}} \geq C\)

The percentile criterion is self-calibrating: it fires when the current state is anomalously close to a minimum relative to recent experience, regardless of the problem’s absolute energy scale. This makes COO robust across problems with very different objective magnitudes.

Phase 2 — Ombra (Dissipative Convergence)

When triggered, the Ombra phase uses the conformal Störmer-Verlet (O-SV) integrator — a geometrically motivated damped integrator:

\[\mu = e^{-\gamma h/2}, \quad v_{n+1/2} = \mu v_n - \frac{h}{2}\nabla f(u_n), \quad u_{n+1} = u_n + h\, v_{n+1/2}, \quad v_{n+1} = \mu\, v_{n+1/2} - \frac{h}{2}\nabla f(u_{n+1})\]

The damping factor \(\mu \in (0,1)\) removes kinetic energy at each step. Theorem 3 establishes the exponential rate \(f(u_n) - f^* \leq H_0 \cdot r^n\) where \(r = (1-\varepsilon h/2)/(1+\varepsilon h/2) \in (0,1)\).

The Lumen resumes from its exact pre-Ombra state after the Ombra sub-call returns — the two phases are fully independent. The Lumen’s Hamiltonian is preserved through every spawn-and-return cycle.

The Key Insight in Theorem 2

The Lyapunov function from Chen et al. (2022), \[V_k = (k+a)^2\bigl(f(x_k) - f^*\bigr) + \frac{1}{2s}\left\|x_k - x^* + \frac{(k+a)s}{2}\nabla f(x_k)\right\|^2\] depends on \(x_k\) and \(\nabla f(x_k)\) — but not directly on \(p_k\). Since Snell-KKT boundary events modify only \(p_k\) while leaving \(x_k\) fixed, they leave \(V_k\) unchanged. The Lyapunov decrease from interior Lumen steps is fully preserved across any number of boundary contacts. This “segment chaining” argument is what allows a single convergence rate to apply to the full algorithm with arbitrarily many boundary interactions.

The Proof Strategy for Theorem 5

The dual convergence proof proceeds in three steps:

  1. Linearize near \(x^*\): reduce the full constrained problem to a local linear equality-constrained quadratic problem near the optimal boundary point.
  2. Import Guminov et al. (2019), Theorem 3: apply the accelerated primal-dual theorem to the linearized problem. Under LICQ (linearly independent active constraint gradients), this gives \(O(1/k^2)\) for both primal and dual averages.
  3. Close the inexact-Uzawa gap: the Lumen performs one leapfrog step per iteration rather than the exact primal minimization the Guminov theorem assumes. This approximation error is \(O(1/k^4)\) per step — absolutely summable — and leaves the asymptotic \(O(1/k^2)\) dual rate unchanged.

Comparison with Existing Methods

Method Dual output Primal rate Architecture
Gradient projection Post-hoc only \(O(\varepsilon^{-1/2})\) Projects momentum at boundary
Augmented Lagrangian Explicit dual update step \(O(\varepsilon^{-1/2})\) Maintains \(\lambda^k\) as algorithmic variable
Interior-point At solution only \(O(n^{3.5}\log(1/\varepsilon))\) Dense linear systems
COO At every boundary event \(O(\varepsilon^{-1/3})\) primal Measures \(\lambda\) from geometry

The distinction from gradient projection is structural: projection discards the approach momentum (replacing it with the tangent-direction vector), while the Snell-KKT operator reflects only the normal component, preserving the tangential component and measuring the normal impulse as the KKT multiplier. Projection treats the constraint boundary as an obstacle; COO treats it as a measuring instrument.

The distinction from augmented Lagrangian is categorical: augmented Lagrangian multipliers are algorithmic decisions (updated by formula \(\lambda^{k+1} = \lambda^k + \rho c(x^k)\)); COO’s Luminosity Flux multipliers are physical measurements — the elastic impulse coefficients at boundary contacts, computed directly from the trajectory geometry.
